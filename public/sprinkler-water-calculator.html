How sprinkler run time is actually calculated

Evapotranspiration (ET) sets the target: it's how much water your lawn loses to evaporation and plant uptake, usually measured in inches per week (higher in desert summer heat, lower in cooler months). That's the depth of water you need to replace — the whole reason "run your sprinklers for 20 minutes" is a guess and "run your sprinklers to replace 1 inch of ET" is a calculation.

Your sprinkler head's precipitation rate (PR), in inches per hour, converts that target depth into minutes:

Runtime (minutes) = (target depth in inches ÷ precipitation rate in inches/hour) × 60

Different heads apply water at very different rates:

Head typeTypical precipitation rate
Gear-drive rotor~0.5 in/hr
Conventional pop-up spray~1.5–2.0 in/hr
MP Rotator / multi-stream rotary nozzle~0.3–0.4 in/hr
Drip / inline emitter tubingsized in gal/hr per emitter, not in/hr

The last piece is soil type, which caps how fast water can actually soak in before it runs off (the infiltration rate). Clay soil typically absorbs only about 0.1–0.2 in/hr, sandy soil over 1 in/hr, and loam somewhere in between. If a head's precipitation rate is higher than the soil can absorb — a common mismatch on clay — the fix isn't a shorter runtime, it's cycle and soak: splitting the same total runtime into two to four shorter cycles with a 30–60 minute soak break between them, so the water has time to soak in instead of running off the lawn onto the driveway or sidewalk. Drip zones skip precipitation-rate math entirely — their "runtime" is about matching total emitter gallons-per-hour to the plant's actual water need, which is why lawn zones and drip zones need separate schedules.

Sprinkler run time FAQ

How long should I run my sprinklers?

It depends on your sprinkler head's precipitation rate and how much water your lawn needs that week (its evapotranspiration, or ET, rate), not a fixed number of minutes. Divide your target watering depth in inches by your head's precipitation rate in inches per hour, then multiply by 60 to get minutes. A gear rotor applying 0.5 inches per hour needs about 120 minutes to deliver 1 inch of water; a lower-precipitation-rate head needs longer per session but often wastes less water overall.

How do I calculate sprinkler run time for my soil type?

Start with the same runtime formula (target inches divided by precipitation rate, times 60), then check it against your soil's infiltration rate — how fast water can actually soak in before it runs off. Clay soil typically absorbs only about 0.1-0.2 inches per hour, sandy soil over 1 inch per hour, and loam somewhere in between. If your head's precipitation rate is higher than your soil can absorb, split the total runtime into two to four shorter cycles with a 30-60 minute soak break between them (cycle and soak) instead of running it all at once.

Do water-saving sprinkler heads really save money?

Usually, yes — but the savings come from efficiency, not shorter runtimes. Water-saving heads like MP rotators and high-efficiency spray nozzles apply water more slowly and evenly, cutting runoff and wind drift, so a lawn needs less total water even though each session can run longer. In our illustrative example above, switching a 500 sq ft lawn zone from conventional gear rotors to water-saving heads cut annual water use by about 31%.

What's cycle and soak, and when do I need it?

Cycle and soak means splitting one long watering session into several shorter ones with a break in between, so water has time to soak into the soil instead of running off the surface or down the driveway. It matters most on clay soil, slopes, and compacted ground, where a sprinkler's precipitation rate outpaces how fast the soil can absorb water. Sandy soil rarely needs it since it drains quickly on its own.
